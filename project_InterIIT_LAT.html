Introduction

I led the Aerodynamic design of IIT Kanpur’s next FSAE car from end-to-end, developing the team’s first full aero package in over five years.

Vortex structures formed by flow over the rear wing

-CFD(velocity), Side View

The goal was not just to generate down-force (while minimizing drag), but to control vortical structures for robustness across various ride heights and operating conditions.

A mix of analytical models, 2D and 3D CFD, and iterative vortex-driven geometry development was used to design and validate the package.

Robust, manufacturable geometries were prioritized so the concepts could be implemented and iterated reliably by the team.

Approach

This aero package was developed around vortex control rather than brute-force camber. Across both front and rear wings, geometry was used to create coherent streamwise vortices that seal low-pressure regions, stabilize suction surface, and reduce sensitivity to ride height and flow disturbances.

  • Target Selection

    Lap-time sensitivity analysis and OptimumG-based estimates were used to define downforce and balance targets.

  • →

  • Airfoil & Element selection

    Multi-element airfoil theory and extensive 2D CFD were used to determine flap and main-element geometry. Chord split, gap, overlap and relative angle were optimized for lift and pressure recovery.

  • →

  • 3D Plain Wing

    The validated 2D sections were simply extruded into a 3D-wing and paired with simple rectangular endplates. This produced a clean aerodynamic baseline.

  • →

  • Endplate Optimization & Vortex Control (Front & Rear-wings)

    A sequence of geometric modifications were tested to manipulate the strength, position and formation of the various vortices. The goal was to reduce over-wing pressure loss while increasing under-wing suction.

  • →

  • Integration and System-Level Intent

    The front and rear wings were developed as parts of a single aerodynamic system. Each geometry was engineered to produce coherent, stable vortices.

A P P R O A C H

Front-wing

{Vortex-sealed Under-Wing Concept}

The front wing was designed as a vortex-controlled suction system, where a stable endplate-generated vortex seals the low-pressure region against the surroundings.

Early concepts showed that strong outboard loading and aggressive endplate curvature produced a powerful vortex, but also injected large amounts of dirty-air. This contaminated the flow field downstream and destabilized the sealing mechanism.

Early front-wing vortex concept(design notebook)

-These sketches show why tubular outboard section was needed and capture how it was expected to entrain and regulate the ground-sealing vortex, and why uncontrolled growth would lead to vortex breakdown and loss of downforce.

To understand this further, the wing was decomposed into its vortex-producing elements:

The outer endplate, the lower edge near the ground, and the interaction between the two.

CFD & Sketch-based reasoning showed that uncontrolled strengthening of this vortex leads to vortex breakdown → the sudden expansion and loss of coherence that causes the seal to collapse.

The sketches capture two critical observations:

  • When the vortex grows too strong, it "blows up" - it expands, detaches from the ground and loses its ability to seal the low pressure under the wing.

  • If external flow is allowed to leak into the vortex core, it accelerates the breakdown.

This is why a simple Gurney-based or flap-based approach was rejected for the front wing.
A Gurney would have increased circulation, but without vortex containment, it would have driven the sealing vortex into an unstable regime.
After several iterations, the solution emerged: a two-step endplate system.

  • Tubular cross-section that generated the strong, initial sealing vortex.

  • Flat-plate that allowed this vortex to leak underneath controllably, keeping the vortex contained while still maintaining the seal.

F R O N T W I N G

Rear-wing

{Endplate-Controlled Vortex System}

The rear wing was developed around a single idea: Gurney based vortex-control. Use gurneys to control the vortex's position & strength. These vortices otherwise dilute the pressure difference across the wing.

A deliberately simple baseline was used: a single-element wing extruded between two rectangular endplates. This isolates the dominant three-dimensional effect of rear wings:
the lower & upper-edge endplate vortices.

Early Rear-wing Concepts(Design Notebook)

These sketches show why Gurney-based endplate modifications were needed and help motivate key design decisions.
It also demonstrates the further need for improvement since the major culprit- lower edge vortex- is still left unchecked.

In this baseline case, the large pressure difference between the upper and lower surfaces drives a vortex at the bottom edge of the endplate.

This vortex acts as a pressure short-circuit, pulling high-energy suction-side flow into its core and reducing the effective pressure difference that produces downforce.

The goal was therefore not to eliminate vortices, but to control how and where they form.

The first modification was a flat gurney added to the trailing edge of the endplate.

A gurney increases the effective camber of a lifting surface by creating a stagnation region on one side and a separated region on the other.

Applied to the endplate, it reinforces the low-pressure under-wing(this is because circulation across the endplate is increased) & weakens the upper vortex. However, the lower vortex is strengthened (Katz, RaceCar Aerodynamics).

CFD showed that while near-endplate downforce increased slightly, the stronger vortex pulled even more low-pressure flow off the wing, increasing leakage.

This revealed a critical insight:

A stronger vortex is not automatically beneficial - if it remains attached to the wing, it degrades suction.

Flat-gurney endplate (CFD, bottom view)

The vortex is stronger but remains coupled to the suction surface, increasing pressure leakage.

Top-cut endplate (CFD, Back view)

Removing endplate area above the wing suppresses unwanted over-wing suction.

The flat gurney increased suction on the upper surface of the wing by enabling strong low-pressure regions to form over the endplate.

To counter this, the top of the endplate was cut away in a triangular profile.

This reduced pressure drop above the wing while preserving the ability of the endplate to shape the lower vortex.
The final evolution was to curve the gurney.

This does not weaken the vortex - it changes its trajectory. CFD showed that the curved gurney caused the vortex to:

  • Form earlier
  • Roll away from the wing
  • And detach into the wake instead of remaining coupled to the suction surface

Pressure contours confirmed that the low-pressure core was now spatially separated from the wing, preventing it from entraining suction-side flow.

In effect, the curved gurney acted as a vortex management device, not a lift device.

R E A R W I N G

Computational Scope

All aerodynamic development shown here was performed on isolated front and rear wings. This was a deliberate choice:

Isolating each wing makes the dominant vortex structures and pressure-leakage mechanisms visible and controllable, which is not possible once the wake of one component contaminates the other.

In a full car, the front wing’s wake will inevitably interact with the rear wing, and this interaction can shift vortex position, strength, and stability.

Those effects were not captured here due to computational limits, but the geometries were designed so their vortex functions remain compatible: the front wing produces a structured, ground-attached sealing vortex, while the rear wing is shaped to shed and eject vortices into the wake rather than feed them back into the suction surfaces.

This makes the concepts interaction-ready - they are not tuned to a fragile CFD setup, but to survive when placed on a real car.
